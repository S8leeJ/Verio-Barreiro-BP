Verio Barreira
Verio Barreira is an innovative barrier system designed to sustain the coasts of rivers and combat erosion, particularly in areas with poor infrastructure and close to urban centers. By stabilizing riverbanks and reducing sediment runoff, Verio Barreira protects communities from flooding and infrastructure damage, ensuring cleaner water and a safer environment.

Our Prototype

The Verio Barreira is an innovative erosion control barrier designed to stabilize riverbanks and protect coastal regions from the adverse effects of erosion and flooding.

It features a composite structure combining durable materials like reinforced concrete and eco-friendly geotextiles with natural elements such as rocks and vegetation. The concrete provides strength and longevity, while the geotextiles enhance soil stability and water permeability. The integration of native plants not only helps anchor the soil but also restores natural habitats, promoting biodiversity. The barrier is modular and adaptable, allowing for customized configurations to suit various environmental conditions and site-specific requirements. Its design prioritizes both structural integrity and ecological harmony, ensuring effective erosion control while minimizing environmental impact.

Data that is collected

When focusing on factors directly in the water for measuring river erosion, key aspects include flow velocity, discharge, sediment load, water depth, turbidity, bed material composition, shear stress, and channel slope. Measuring flow velocity with flow meters or Doppler devices helps understand the energy available for erosion, while recording discharge, the volume of water flowing per unit time, influences the river's capacity to erode and transport sediments. Quantifying sediment load, including suspended and bedload sediments, and monitoring changes in water depth can indicate erosion or deposition within the river channel. Measuring turbidity helps assess the concentration of suspended sediments.

Analyzing bed material composition, including size, type, and distribution of materials on the riverbed, is crucial as different materials erode at different rates. Calculating shear stress, the force exerted by flowing water on the riverbed and banks, and measuring channel slope to understand changes in water velocity and erosive power along the river are also essential. These measurements provide a comprehensive understanding of the physical processes of erosion occurring within the water body itself.
